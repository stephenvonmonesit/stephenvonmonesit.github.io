01 — The Psychology

What actually pulls us toward the wrong people

Research points to five overlapping forces: early attachment, trauma, misplaced confidence, environment, and culture. None of them are destiny — but together, they explain why the pattern repeats.

i.

Familiar chaos feels like love

Attachment theory — first mapped by John Bowlby and Mary Ainsworth — shows that children raised with inconsistent affection or unpredictable caregivers often grow into adults with anxious or insecure attachment styles.

As adults, some become drawn to emotionally unavailable partners simply because unpredictability feels familiar. When affection and rejection are on a loop, the brain starts reading intensity as intimacy. A calm, steady relationship can even feel "boring" by comparison — not because it's lacking, but because it's unfamiliar.

Trauma doesn't cause poor choices. It distorts what feels familiar.

ii.

Confidence isn't character

Charisma, boldness, and social dominance are magnetic — and easy to mistake for integrity. Some people are simply skilled at impression management, or at love bombing: fast, excessive affection designed to build emotional dependence early.

Decades of film and fiction have romanticized this exact profile — the emotionally unavailable man who "changes because of love." Research on Dark Triad traits (narcissism, Machiavellianism, psychopathy) finds they can boost short-term attraction through sheer confidence, while consistently predicting worse long-term outcomes: manipulation, infidelity, lower satisfaction. The spark is often real. It's just not evidence of anything durable.

iii.

What we learn to accept

People tend to normalize what they repeatedly witness — fighting, control, or emotional volatility can start to look like a normal part of relationships, not because anyone wants pain, but because it became familiar.

Self-worth compounds this. People who don't believe they deserve healthy love often tolerate far less than they should, reasoning "maybe this is the best I can get" or "he'll change." Healthy self-esteem tends to raise the bar; low self-esteem quietly lowers it.

iv.

The fixer trap

"I can fix him" is one of the most common — and costly — beliefs in unhealthy relationships. It replaces someone's actual pattern of behavior with their potential. But love can't substitute for accountability; people change when they choose to, not because someone sacrifices enough to earn it.

Uncertainty itself plays a role, too. Not knowing where you stand activates dopamine in a way that can feel like chemistry — which is part of why unhealthy relationships can be so hard to walk away from, even when they hurt.

v.

Culture rewards the wrong traits

Modern culture still glamorizes emotional unavailability, jealousy as "proof of love," and playing hard to get — while genuinely kind, steady men get dismissed as boring or predictable. Some cultural scripts go further, teaching women to endure silently and sacrifice their needs to preserve a relationship.

Mutual respect strengthens a relationship. One-sided sacrifice quietly erodes it.

What research says

  • Insecure attachment is linked to greater relationship instability.
  • Childhood exposure to family conflict predicts greater tolerance of unhealthy relationship behavior later.
  • Lower self-esteem is associated with staying longer in unsatisfying or harmful relationships.
  • Narcissistic and psychopathic traits can raise initial attraction but predict worse long-term relationship quality.
  • Emotional abuse tends to follow recognizable cycles, which is part of why it's so hard to leave.

Key takeaway

Being drawn to intensity does not mean someone is safe. It can simply mean the nervous system learned to mistake chaos for closeness.

Gentle shift

The goal is not to shame anyone for past choices, but to widen the definition of what love can feel like.
